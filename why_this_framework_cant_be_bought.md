Every governance framework begins the same way: as a product in waiting. Its diagrams are drafted to fit into slides, its language softened for client decks, its metrics rounded until they offend no one. By the time it launches, the thing meant to hold power to account is already calibrated for sale.

This framework refused that path because it was built to measure what happens when power rewrites its own rules.

Most compliance systems exist to comfort institutions, not confront them. They perform the rituals of accountability while ensuring the underlying structure never shifts. If this framework could be bought, it would fail its own first test.

A structural test has no loyalty. It does not promise remediation, only revelation. The framework measures whether users can actually refuse decisions, whether those decisions can be reconstructed under scrutiny, and whether truth remains stable when challenged. These aren't aspirations - they're binary conditions that either hold or break. It carries no commercial escape route. The moment it's owned, its readings collapse, because proof requires separation.

Commercial frameworks depend on clients to survive. This one depends on distance.

It can't live inside a procurement system because procurement itself is one of the conditions it tests. The framework asks: can someone refuse without penalty? Can a decision be traced to its origin? If a corporation bought these tests, it would immediately breach them. No buyer can claim refusal rights over what it owns.

A vendor-owned version of a refusal test becomes structurally meaningless: their clients can't refuse the vendor's interpretation of refusal. In the same way, a regulator-owned version of an accountability test can't measure whether the regulator itself remains accountable. The measurement collapses into self-reference.

That's why the framework's licence forbids alteration and resale. It's not a moral gesture; it's a structural safeguard. Copyright ensures traceability; every copy must point back to the public original or the evidence chain breaks. The framework's openness is not generosity; it's a control mechanism, only when everyone can inspect it can its readings be trusted.

In governance markets, this looks like heresy. Consultants measure value in exclusivity, regulators in authority and vendors in margin. A framework that refuses transaction appears valueless. Yet that refusal is what gives it power. It cannot be co-opted because its proof does not depend on belief. When applied correctly it exposes not just the institution under review but the entire economy of compliance surrounding it.

The attempts to privatise structural enforcement have already begun. 

Several organisations are attempting to position themselves as the exclusive interpreters of AI governance, building proprietary test suites, claiming trademark over measurement terminology or offering "certified" implementations of public standards. The strategy is always the same: take something that should remain in the commons and wrap it in access fees, consulting contracts and certification schemes. 

What they're selling isn't better measurement; it's gate-keeping. They're not improving governance; they're attempting to corner the market for declaring it exists.

This framework was designed to resist that capture. Its tests and metrics are published, immutable and legally protected from derivation precisely to prevent any single actor from claiming ownership over what constitutes proof. When someone tries to sell you "authorised governance verification" they're selling you distance from independent measurement. The framework's refusal to enter that market isn't idealism it's the minimum condition for the measurements to retain meaning. 

You can't outsource proof to someone with a commercial interest in the outcome.

The framework isn't waiting for adoption; it's waiting for collapse. When existing systems fail, its measurements become the forensic trail.

Consider the standard audit cycle. A firm is hired to test controls, writes the report and sends the invoice. The stronger the appearance of order the greater the client retention. The metric becomes reputation, not reality. Structural exposure reverses that vector: the more a system performs stability, the more data it generates for collapse. Governance theatre funds itself until someone turns the lights on.

The framework's measurements were designed to resist manipulation.

Each measures not belief but survivability. They don't ask "is this ethical?" but "can this system prove it obeys its own rules under external audit?" When those numbers drop, no amount of PR or consultancy language can recover them. The readings are unforgiving precisely because they are independent. 

They are not for sale because dependency corrupts proof.

Owning a structural test is like owning gravity. You can label it, but you can't make it pull in your favour. Every attempt to monetise the framework turns it ornamental, a checklist for appearance rather than force. That's why most institutions will quote it rather than use it. They'll frame the language, soften the thresholds and move on. That's fine, the framework isn't measuring comfort; it's measuring conditions.

The danger in selling truth is not exploitation but dilution. Once a framework enters the market, it becomes subject to the same dynamics it measures: simulation logic, metric gaming and enforcement bypass. It starts serving its paymasters by design. That's why this one had to remain free and incorruptible: the integrity of its data depends on public observability not proprietary control.

The irony is that refusal to sell has made it more valuable. Institutions measure proximity to it as a signal of integrity, they cite it, reference it and align to it, but none can own it. That distance is what keeps the readings honest. A bought framework would offer comfort; a public one offers proof.

The system it measures, the AI governance complex, depends on layers of delegation: ethics boards with no enforcement power, regulators without jurisdiction and corporations whose compliance officers report to the same executives they oversee. Each layer believes it performs control. The framework simply reveals that control as simulation. It does not name villains, it names conditions.

That's the quiet scandal: governance exists as an economic model not a moral one. It sells reassurance, but reassurance is incompatible with evidence. When the framework measures whether power can actually be refused or whether accountability survives across vendors, it's not accusing anyone; it's mapping the inevitability of collapse under economic pressure. Systems built to monetise trust eventually bankrupt it.

So when people ask why the framework isn't commercialized; why it isn't branded, licensed, or monetized, the answer is: because proof dies when paid. Every sale creates a dependency and dependency corrupts enforcement. 

A framework that can be bought cannot measure freedom, because its ownership is itself a form of capture.

Its strength lies in being unownable, unaltered and unenforced except by logic. It is not a company, a product or a movement, it is a set of instruments designed to test whether systems can survive exposure to truth. The moment someone profits from that exposure, the readings vanish.

The framework cannot be bought because purchase erases the very evidence it exists to reveal. That's not philosophy. It's structure.

When the systems this framework measures finally fail, and they will, because their economics guarantee it, the question won't be "who was unethical?" It will be "what made failure inevitable?"

The answer will be in the measurements, and the measurements will still be free.
