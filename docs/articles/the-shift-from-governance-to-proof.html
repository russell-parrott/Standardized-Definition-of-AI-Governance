Can One Challenged Decision Actually Be Shown?

When a regulator, court or insurer examines a disputed outcome the discussion narrows very quickly. Broad descriptions of governance stop mattering on their own. The focus shifts to one decision affecting one person at one point in time. What happened, who allowed it, what records existed before it occurred, what evidence shows the outcome actually took place and what basis permitted it at that moment.

Many organisations are less prepared for that shift than they appear. Their governance material is often designed to describe structures, responsibilities and oversight in general terms. Scrutiny, however, increasingly operates at the level of the individual case rather than the level of the framework.

Regulators, and in some areas courts, are becoming less interested in whether governance existed in broad terms and more interested in whether a specific decision can actually be reconstructed from records and evidence that already exist. That distinction matters because it changes the level at which scrutiny operates. Instead of examining governance as a general organisational condition, scrutiny increasingly focuses on one outcome affecting one person at one point in time.

This is the position DAREB starts from. DAREB does not examine systems in the abstract. It examines whether a single challenged decision can be shown from beginning to end. That means being able to identify the decision itself, the authority that allowed it, the record in which that authority existed, the evidence showing the outcome occurred and the basis that made the decision permitted at the time.

Recent regulatory activity increasingly points in this direction.

One of the clearest examples is the Italian data protection authority’s enforcement action against Luka Inc. concerning the Replika chatbot. The significance of the case is not that it involved artificial intelligence. The significance is that the regulator examined what actually happened in practice rather than accepting governance descriptions at face value. The authority focused on whether age verification mechanisms genuinely existed and operated during the relevant period. In practical terms, the regulator was not asking whether the organisation claimed to care about age verification. It was asking whether the mechanism could be shown to have existed and functioned in reality at the time the relevant interactions occurred.

That distinction is important because governance language often describes intentions while enforcement examines events. A policy stating that controls should exist is not the same thing as evidence showing that those controls operated in a specific case. The closer scrutiny moves toward real outcomes and provable operation, the less protection broad governance language provides on its own.

A similar movement can be seen in financial regulation through the UK Financial Conduct Authority’s increasing focus on individual accountability under the Senior Managers and Certification Regime. The importance of this development is frequently understated. The regime does not treat accountability as something held abstractly by “the organisation” in general terms. It attaches responsibility to named individuals holding defined authority. That changes the practical nature of regulatory scrutiny because the central question becomes much narrower and much more concrete.

The issue is no longer simply whether governance processes existed around a system. The issue becomes whether a named person or body held the authority to permit the type of decision that occurred at the time it occurred. That sits directly inside the Authority element of DAREB. Authority does not exist for accountability purposes merely because a process operated or because a system produced an output. Authority exists only where a person or recognised body can be shown to have held the power to permit that outcome in that case.

The EU AI Act also moves in a related direction through its record-keeping and logging obligations for high-risk systems. The Act does not merely ask organisations to publish principles or describe governance structures at a high level. It creates obligations around traceability, monitoring and the ability to examine system operation after the fact. While the legislation does not literally require reconstruction of every individual decision in every circumstance, the practical effect is similar. Traceability only becomes meaningful if it allows specific outcomes to be examined later in a way that connects decisions, authority, evidence and basis together coherently.

This is where much governance language becomes unstable under scrutiny. Traceability is often described as though the existence of logs automatically creates accountability. In practice, logs only show activity. They do not necessarily show authority. They do not necessarily show whether the decision was permitted. They do not necessarily show whether the basis existed before the decision occurred. DAREB narrows the issue by asking whether the full evidential chain around a specific decision can actually be demonstrated rather than merely described.

The position within the courts is more uneven, but important changes are still visible. The end of Chevron deference in the United States through Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo does not itself create case-level scrutiny in the strict DAREB sense. Courts are not suddenly reconstructing every administrative decision individually. However, the decision does increase pressure on agencies to justify interpretations and actions through clearer reasoning and stronger records rather than relying on judicial deference to ambiguous statutory interpretation.

That distinction is important because the shift is not toward universal evidential reconstruction but toward reduced tolerance for unsupported institutional assertion. Agencies increasingly need to demonstrate why actions were justified rather than assuming courts will defer to their interpretation automatically. The practical effect is a greater emphasis on records, reasoning and demonstrable basis.

A similar pattern appears in judicial treatment of reliance interests within administrative law. Courts increasingly expect agencies to address the practical effects of decisions on affected people rather than relying only on abstract policy authority. Again, this does not yet amount to full case-level evidential scrutiny across all legal domains. However, it does reflect movement toward closer examination of how specific decisions affect specific people and whether those effects were properly considered and justified.

Technology is also changing evidential expectations. The increasing use of video evidence has altered how some appellate courts examine disputed events because direct visual records can challenge or contradict written descriptions of what occurred. In some circumstances, courts have been willing to examine video evidence independently rather than relying entirely on lower-court characterisations of events.

The broader significance is not limited to video itself. The more direct evidence exists, the weaker purely descriptive governance narratives become. Once an event can be replayed, reconstructed or directly examined, institutional explanations alone carry less weight. The existence of direct evidence changes the standard of scrutiny because it shifts attention from description toward demonstrable fact.

At the same time, the position should not be overstated. Courts still show substantial deference in many areas and some recent decisions continue to apply highly deferential standards that make certain forms of challenge extremely difficult. Constitutional levels of scrutiny also remain central to judicial reasoning across many legal contexts. There is no universal legal movement toward intensive reconstruction of every challenged decision.

The stronger and more defensible conclusion is narrower than that.

The legal and regulatory environment is becoming less tolerant of accountability structures that cannot answer specific evidential questions about individual decisions. That movement is already visible in data protection enforcement, financial regulation and AI-related traceability obligations. The practical effect is that broad governance descriptions increasingly become insufficient once scrutiny narrows to one identifiable outcome.

An organisation may possess governance frameworks, oversight committees, principles and policies. None of those disappear. However, when a decision is challenged, examination increasingly compresses toward a much smaller set of questions. What happened. Who allowed it. What record existed before the decision. What evidence shows the outcome occurred. What basis permitted it at that moment.

That is the environment DAREB is designed for. Not a world in which every court applies identical standards of evidential reconstruction and not a world in which all governance review has disappeared, but a world in which accountability increasingly depends on whether a specific decision can actually be shown from records and evidence that already exist.
