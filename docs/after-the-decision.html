After the Decision

The Reconstruction Circle essays examine what happens after AI decisions are challenged and organisations are forced to explain what actually occurred. They are not about future promises, ethical principles or governance theory in the abstract. They focus on something much narrower and much harder: what can still be shown later, using surviving records and evidence, once scrutiny begins.

Across different settings, oversight, logging, vendors, human review, retention and operational pressure, they follow the same pattern repeatedly. Organisations often believe they are accountable because governance structures exist. These essays examine what happens later, when someone tries to reconstruct what actually happened and discovers that part of the evidence no longer exists.

The recurring theme is that accountability is not created by policies alone. It depends on whether decisions can still be reconstructed afterwards using evidence that already existed at the time.

The Reconstruction Circle

The Reconstruction Circle is a small private reading circle built around one question: what can still be proven after an AI-assisted decision is challenged.

The work is not publically published on LinkedIn, Amazon or anywhere else. Members receive one to two private papers monthly. Each is usually between 3,000 and 5,000 words and examines one specific accountability problem in depth.

The focus is not abstract AI ethics, governance theatre or generic responsible AI discussion but what organisations can still prove after a contested decision.

Recent papers have explored hidden AI arriving through ordinary software updates. Why EU governance structures often fail under US-style litigation scrutiny. And how legal departments frequently discover that accountability architecture was fixed long before they ever became involved.

Because eventually somebody asks: who decided, why and where is the evidence?

This is not early access to public writing. The work remains private to the group.

Members can influence which accountability problems are examined next, but the structure is deliberately simple. There are no calls, no discussion forums, no networking obligations and no consulting attached to it. The work arrives. You read it in your own time.

The intention is not to build an online community. It is to create a small private space for serious accountability analysis outside the pressures of institutional language, platform algorithms and performative AI discourse.

The group is intentionally limited in size.
