Questions About What Can Be Shown After a Decision Is Challenged.

The questions on this page all lead back to one issue: what can actually be shown after an automated decision is challenged.

The focus throughout is practical and evidential. Not whether governance exists in theory, but whether a single decision can still be reconstructed afterwards using records and evidence that already exist.

This site examines one practical question: what can be proven after an AI-assisted or automated decision is challenged? It is about accountability after the event. It looks at whether a decision can still be reconstructed from records, evidence, authority and surviving information. It is not a legal site. It is not a compliance site. It is not a governance framework. It is an educational reference site about proof, responsibility and accountability.
Reconstructable accountability means being able to show what happened after a decision is challenged. That means being able to reconstruct one specific decision from records that already exist. It asks whether the organisation can still show what was decided, what system was involved, who had authority, what evidence survives and why the outcome was allowed at that time. The focus is not whether an organisation says it had governance. The focus is whether it can show what actually happened.
DAREB is a public accountability test built around five things that must be capable of being shown when one automated or AI-assisted decision is challenged. Those five things are Decision, Authority, Record, Evidence and Basis. DAREB asks whether one decision can be shown clearly enough to support accountability after the event. It does not tell organisations how to run AI systems. It examines whether accountability can be proven from what survives.
The Governance Mirage is an article about the gap between the appearance of AI governance and the reality of reconstructable accountability. It explains how organisations can look well governed because they have policies, committees, risk registers and oversight processes, while still being unable to reconstruct one contested AI-assisted decision later. The article argues that governance appearance and accountability proof are not the same thing.
The Governance Mirage explains the wider problem. DAREB gives that problem a sharper accountability test. The article describes why organisations may appear to have control while lacking the evidence needed to prove what happened. DAREB then asks the direct question underneath that problem: can one challenged decision actually be shown? They are connected, but they do different jobs. The Governance Mirage explains the institutional weakness. DAREB tests whether accountability can survive a challenge.

It contains linked accountability conditions, evidence requirements and exposure triggers. Its purpose is to explain what must be capable of being shown for accountability to exist. Governance theatre is the appearance of control without the proof needed to support accountability. The Governance Mirage explores this problem in detail.
Governance appearance means the visible signs that an organisation is in control. These may include policies, principles, dashboards, committees, reports and risk registers. Those things may be useful, but they do not automatically prove what happened in one disputed decision. What does “accountability proof” mean? Accountability proof means the surviving records and evidence that allow a decision to be reconstructed after it is challenged. It is not enough to say that responsibility existed. The question is whether responsibility can be shown.
