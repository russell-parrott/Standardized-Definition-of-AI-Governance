THE MIRAGE DAREB ARTICLES FAQ LICENSING ABOUT CONTACT

Most organisations believe they can govern AI because they can describe it.

From the outside, governance structures create the impression that accountability already exists. The organisation appears organised, supervised and controlled.

The central question is not whether governance structures exist, but whether a contested AI-assisted decision can still be reconstructed, evidenced and defended months or years after it occurred.

That problem becomes harder as responsibility spreads across systems, vendors, automated processes and operational environments that are difficult to reconstruct afterwards. System behaviour changes over time, evidence may not survive while human oversight might formally exist but be weak in practice.

This site examines reconstructable accountability, evidential proof and the growing gap between governance appearance and what can actually be shown after automated or AI-assisted decisions are challenged.

The Governance Mirage - Why governance appearance and reconstructable accountability are not the same thing.

Many boards believe their AI is well governed because they have checked the traditional boxes, writing policies, forming committees and updating risk registers. However, this can become "governance theatre": a performance of oversight providing a false sense of security while the organisation’s actual accountability remains dangerously fragile underneath.

This article examines how several factors create this "mirage":

  • The "Category Mistake": Boards often try to force AI into existing silos like IT, Finance, or Legal. In reality, AI responsibility is scattered across vendors, data sources and automated processes that no single function truly controls.
  • Pressure vs. Proof: Organisations are often pushed by commercial and psychological pressures to prioritise the immediate "wins" of AI, like speed and efficiency, over the expensive, invisible work of building durable audit trails.
  • Procedural Reassurance vs. Evidential Survivability: Traditional governance focuses on whether a process was followed. Real accountability depends on reconstructability: the ability to explain and defend one specific AI-assisted decision long after it occurred, even where systems, models or vendors have changed afterwards.

DAREB© - What must be shown for a decision to stand.

DAREB© starts with a single decision and stays with it. It does not look at systems or ask how they are intended to work. It takes one decision and sets out what must be present when examined for it to hold.

Every challenged decision eventually reaches the same question: can you show exactly what happened, who allowed it, and why it was permitted at the time. Not in general terms. Not as a process diagram. For this decision, affecting this person, at this moment.

Five things must be shown and each must be tied to that individual case:

  • Decision – What was done, to whom, and at what exact time.
  • Authority – The named person or body that allowed that outcome to happen at that time.
  • Record – Where that authority is written down and can be produced, and which existed before the decision was made.
  • Evidence – That the decision and its effect on the person actually happened, captured at the time.
  • Basis – The rule, law or agreement that applied at the time and permitted the decision.

If any one of these cannot be shown from records and evidence that already exist, the decision does not hold. What is missing is treated as absent.

The single decision question

Take a single decision made by your system and assume it is challenged tomorrow and answer the following question. Note: each part of the question must be supported by evidence, not just described:
Without accessing the system's internal constructs can you show the decision that was actually made and what happened as a result. Who, as a named person or organisation, authorised it. Where that authority is recorded, what it was based on at the time the decision occurred, and what legal, regulatory or contractual basis applied and allowed it to go ahead?
This is what regulators, courts, insurers and affected individuals require.

Across laws such as the EU AI Act, the Product Liability Directive, and disclosure rules in the UK and US, the position is consistent. If you cannot show the evidence, you cannot prove how the decision was made, who was responsible, or why it was allowed.

At that point, the decision cannot be relied on. It may be rejected, reversed or treated as unsupported. The focus then shifts from what the system did to why it was allowed to operate without a provable basis.

Where governance, responsibility and accountability actually sit.

This also shows how governance, responsibility and accountability fit together in practice.
  • Governance sets the conditions before the decision: who may act, under what rules and how that is recorded.
  • Responsibility sits at the moment of the decision and attaches to the named person or body that had the authority to permit that outcome in that specific case. It does not sit with a system or a process.
  • Accountability exists afterwards, when a decision is challenged and each element must be shown from records and evidence that already exist. If any part is missing, the chain breaks.

These are not separate ideas. They are the same chain seen at different points in time. Governance makes responsibility possible. Accountability tests whether both can be shown.
