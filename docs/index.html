    Standardized Definition of AI Governance

    Most organizations claim their AI systems are governed. Few can prove it. Fewer still can measure what ungoverned AI actually costs.

    AI Governance is the system of principles, policies, processes and accountability mechanisms that direct and control the entire lifecycle of artificial-intelligence systems, from design through decommissioning, to ensure that they operate lawfully, safely and in alignment with human values, stakeholder rights and societal objectives.

    AI Governance exists to:

    • Align AI outcomes with declared legal, organizational and societal objectives
    • Preserve meaningful human agency and authority
    • Prevent, detect and remedy harm across technical, social and systemic dimensions

    Making AI Governance Measurable - The Framework

    The Standardized Definition of AI Governance converts abstract ethics into concrete structure. It defines governance not as what you promise, but as what your systems can actually do when tested under pressure, and what you lose when they fail.

    Three questions expose the reality:

    • Can your system be controlled? (15 Structural Tests + 4 metrics)
    • Can its outputs be trusted? (5 Epistemic Tests + 1 metric)
    • Does governance survive across boundaries? (5 Systemic Tests + 2 metrics)
    Pass all 25 tests, maintain healthy scores across 7 continuous metrics, and you've demonstrated something rare: provable governance.

    The Economic Reality

    Unverifiable AI governance costs economies approximately 0.1% of GDP annually through three measurable loss channels:
    • Job displacement without transition (NTAD) - lost wages when automation occurs without retraining
    • Public cleanup costs (PRE) - taxpayer money fixing preventable AI failures
    • Trust slowdown (VAG) - delayed adoption because safety can't be proven
    Real Numbers:
    • Singapore's financial sector: US$77-160M in annual losses
    • Germany: €4.3B annually
    • United Kingdom: £2.9B annually
