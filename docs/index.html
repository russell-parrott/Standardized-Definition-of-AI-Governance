Can AI-Assisted Decisions Still Be Proven Years Later?

Most organisations believe they are accountable because they have AI audit trails, logging, governance policies and compliance documentation. They talk about AI accountability as if it exists automatically. However, visible governance does not necessarily mean they can later reconstruct and defend one contested AI-assisted decision.

I write books, papers and explanatory material about whether organisations can later show what happened in AI-assisted decisions, who allowed them and what evidence still exists. My work examines if under regulatory, legal or public scrutiny, they can still prove and explain those decisions months or years after they were originally made.

I do not focus on whether organisations can describe governance in general terms while systems operate. I concentrate on whether they can later show exactly what happened in one specific disputed case, who allowed it, what records existed at the time and what evidence still survives now.

Not anti-AI, just honest about what can be proved later. Because eventually somebody asks: who decided, why and where is the evidence?

The Governance Mirage - Why visible governance does not prove accountability

Boards check boxes: policies, committees and risk registers. They believe they have AI governed but this is often governance theatre; oversight that looks real but leaves accountability hollow underneath.

The problem breaks down like this:

  • The category mistake – AI is forced into IT, Legal, or Finance silos. In reality, no single function controls AI. Responsibility is scattered across vendors, data sources, and automated handoffs.
  • Pressure vs. proof – Commercial urgency prioritises AI speed and cost reduction. The invisible work of durable audit trails is deprioritised until too late.
  • Process vs. reconstruction – Traditional governance asks: “Was the rule followed?” Accountability asks: “Can we still explain a specific decision months later, after systems and people have changed?”

Evidential Survivability - Why evidence often disappears long before scrutiny begins

Logs, audit trails, and certifications exist. That does not mean they will survive a real challenge. This is the existence ≠ survivability fallacy.

Consider the lifecycle of evidence under pressure:

  • Present vs. future governance – Today’s oversight asks: “Do we have controls now?” Survivability asks: “Will proof still exist years later, after staff leave, vendors change, logs expire?”
  • Calm archiving vs. hostile attack – Storing evidence is not enough. The real test is whether it survives regulators, courts, or journalists actively trying to break it hunting for gaps, expired records, broken chains of custody.
  • Unnoticed decay – Retention periods expire. Versioning drifts. Vendors delete data. No one notices until the evidence is gone.

The Board’s Blind Spot - Why oversight structures can hide accountability failure

Boards see the surface: policies, ethics principles, immutable logs. They do not see what is underneath. That gap is the blind spot.

Three assumptions lock it in place:

  • Structure = accountability – Boards assume that if governance structures exist, accountability follows. In AI, that is false. Structures can be empty.
  • Green dashboards = safety – Low incident rates and operational confidence feel reassuring. They say nothing about whether a single contested decision could be defended.
  • “We would know” – Boards believe they would be told if accountability were at risk. In practice, no one measures reconstructability until it fails.
The question boards never ask: “Can we defend one specific AI decision under hostile scrutiny a year from now?” Without that question, oversight is just reassurance-seeking.

The Pattern - Why it is clear to an external observer

From the inside, everything looks governed. From the outside, the pattern is obvious. Organisations repeat the same predictable failures.

What an external observer sees:

  • Surface grows, depth erodes – Organisations invest in visible things: certifications, frameworks, policies. Reconstructability — the ability to prove what happened stays invisible and unfunded until it collapses.
  • Predictable surprise – Retention expires. Humans defer to systems. Vendors lose data. Every organisation is surprised; they all thought they were different.
  • No test for the real thing – They test security, accuracy, disaster recovery. They never test whether a past decision can still be reconstructed under scrutiny. Because it is never tested, no one sees the fragility until the first lawsuit or investigation reveals what can no longer be proved.

DAREB© - What must be shown for a decision to stand.

DAREB© starts with a single decision and stays with it. It does not look at systems or ask how they are intended to work. It takes one decision and sets out what must be present when examined for it to hold.

Every challenged decision eventually reaches the same question: can you show exactly what happened, who allowed it, and why it was permitted at the time. Not in general terms. Not as a process diagram. For this decision, affecting this person, at this moment.

Five things must be shown and each must be tied to that individual case:

  • Decision – What was done, to whom, and at what exact time.
  • Authority – The named person or body that allowed that outcome to happen at that time.
  • Record – Where that authority is written down and can be produced, and which existed before the decision was made.
  • Evidence – That the decision and its effect on the person actually happened, captured at the time.
  • Basis – The rule, law or agreement that applied at the time and permitted the decision.
If any one of these cannot be shown from records and evidence that already exist, the decision does not hold. What is missing is treated as absent.
